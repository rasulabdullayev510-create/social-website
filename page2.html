Page 2 — Imperialist Policies and Their Cultural Impact

What did Italy do in Libya, and how did it change Libyan culture and society?

Overview of Colonial Governance

Italy governed Libya as a conquered territory. Colonial administrators made decisions for the population with little to no Libyan input. Policies ranged from land redistribution and forced population movement to restrictions on Islamic practice and Arabic-language education.

Land Seizure and Settler Colonialism

The colonial government classified large areas of agricultural and pastoral land as "state property," dispossessing Libyan families who had farmed it for generations. By the late 1930s, the Italian settler population reached around 110,000. Libyan farmers were pushed into less fertile areas, disrupting entire communities.

The dispossession of land severed communities from ancestral territory, burial sites, and the social identity tied to place.

Violence and Population Control

Between 1929 and 1932, Italian forces conducted a campaign historians describe as a genocide — including mass executions, aerial bombardment, use of poison gas, and mass deportation into desert concentration camps. An estimated 50,000 to 100,000 Libyans died in these camps. The overall death toll is estimated at over half a million.

Suppression of Islamic Culture

The Senussi Brotherhood was targeted — its leaders exiled or executed, its properties seized. Islamic courts were subordinated to Italian civil law. Mosques and religious schools were controlled or closed. A generation of Libyans was cut off from traditional religious and scholarly institutions.

Language and Education Policies

Italian as Official Language

Arabic was marginalized in formal settings, limiting Libyans' access to legal and civic processes.

Restricted Arabic Education

Where schooling existed for Libyans, it was taught in Italian, eroding Arabic literacy across generations.

Cultural Propaganda

Italy promoted the idea that Libya was heir to ancient Roman civilization, erasing centuries of Arab-Berber and Islamic history.

Architecture and Urban Space

Tripoli and Benghazi were redesigned with Roman-style arches and European quarters, physically imposing Italian identity on Libyan cities.

Cultural Resistance

Despite repression, Libyan culture survived through oral tradition, private religious practice, and tribal networks. Resistance leader Omar Mukhtar, executed in 1931, became a powerful national symbol carried forward into the post-colonial era.
