Page 4 — Has the Legacy Been Addressed?

What has Italy done to acknowledge its colonial past, and has the response been efficient?

The Question of Reckoning

Former colonial powers have faced growing pressure to acknowledge their imperial pasts through apologies, reparations, and historical education. Italy's record is mixed — slower and more reluctant than countries like Germany to engage with its colonial record in Libya.

The 2008 Treaty

In 2008, Prime Minister Berlusconi signed the Treaty of Friendship, Partnership, and Cooperation with Gaddafi. Italy formally apologized for colonialism and agreed to pay $5 billion USD over 20 years in infrastructure investment. Berlusconi called colonialism a "historic tragedy" and wore a photo of resistance leader Omar Mukhtar at the signing — a gesture of powerful symbolic meaning for Libyans.

"Italy apologizes for its killing, destruction and repression of the Libyan people during the period of colonial rule." — Silvio Berlusconi, 2008

What the Treaty Did and Did Not Do

What it accomplished

  • First formal Italian apology for colonialism
  • Acknowledged deportations and massacres
  • Committed $5 billion in investment
  • Strengthened Italy-Libya diplomatic relations

What it fell short on

  • Payments went to Gaddafi's government, not victims
  • No individual reparations for survivors
  • No return of cultural artifacts
  • 2011 revolution halted payments — much never delivered
  • No accountability for specific war crimes

After 2011: A Stalled Process

Italy's support for the 2011 NATO intervention effectively froze the treaty. Infrastructure projects halted. With no functioning Libyan government to continue negotiations, the reconciliation process collapsed. Some Libyan groups argue Italy supported the intervention partly to escape its financial obligations under the 2008 treaty.

Italy's Domestic Reckoning: Limited Progress

Within Italy, public awareness of the colonial past remains low. School curricula give limited attention to colonialism in Libya, Ethiopia, Somalia, and Eritrea. Streets in some cities still bear the names of commanders responsible for atrocities. Advocacy groups have pushed for curriculum reform, but progress has been slow.

Libya's Perspective

Omar Mukhtar is a national hero — his face appears on the Libyan ten-dinar banknote. The 1981 film Lion of the Desert, depicting his resistance, was banned in Italy for decades. Many Libyan families carry direct memories of the camps and executions. For many, the 2008 apology was too little, too late, and too entangled with Gaddafi's politics to be a genuine reckoning.

Has the Response Been Efficient?

Honestly: no. The 2008 treaty was a meaningful start but fell apart before implementation. Italy has not educated its population about its colonial past. Artifacts have not been returned. No individual compensation has been paid. Public monuments to perpetrators remain. Real reconciliation remains largely unresolved, both materially and symbolically.
