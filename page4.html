Legacy & Response

How the world has reckoned with Italian colonialism in Libya

Italy's Acknowledgement of Its Colonial Past

For many decades after colonization ended, Italy did not formally acknowledge the harm it caused in Libya. It was not until 2008 that Italy took a significant step, when then Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi signed a Treaty of Friendship with Libyan leader Muammar Gaddafi. In this treaty, Italy officially apologized for its colonial rule and agreed to pay Libya five billion dollars over twenty years as a form of compensation.

This was a historic moment — one of the first times a European country formally apologized and offered financial reparations for colonialism in Africa. However, many historians and Libyan people felt the apology did not go far enough. The agreement was more of a political and economic deal between two governments than a genuine effort to address the deep wounds of colonization.

Was the Response Effective?

The 2008 treaty was seen by many as incomplete. After Gaddafi was removed from power in 2011, the agreement largely fell apart and Italy stopped making payments. This meant that the compensation Libya was promised was never fully delivered. The apology also did not address the full scale of suffering — including the concentration camps, mass executions, and forced displacement of hundreds of thousands of Libyans.

Many Libyans felt that a financial agreement signed between two leaders did not represent true reconciliation. There was no wide public acknowledgement in Italy of what happened, no major education in Italian schools about the colonial period, and no lasting memorial or recognition of Libyan victims.

The Lasting Legacy of Italian Colonialism

The legacy of Italian imperialism in Libya is still felt today. The borders Italy drew, the institutions it failed to build, and the resources it extracted all contributed to the instability Libya faces in the modern era. Tribal divisions that were deepened under colonial rule continue to fuel conflict. The oil-dependent economy that Italy helped create still makes Libya vulnerable.

Culturally, many Libyans lost connection to their language, traditions, and identity during the colonial period. While much of this has been reclaimed, the trauma of colonization — including the deaths of tens of thousands of people in concentration camps and during military operations — has left a mark on Libyan collective memory that has not been fully healed.

Moving Forward

True reconciliation between Italy and Libya would require more than a financial agreement. It would mean full acknowledgement of the crimes committed, education of future generations in Italy about what happened, proper memorials for victims, and ongoing support for Libya's recovery and development. As of today, this kind of deep and lasting reconciliation has not yet been achieved.

The story of Italian imperialism in Libya is a reminder that the effects of colonialism do not end when colonial rule does. They live on in the institutions, economies, borders, and memories of the people who were colonized — and they require genuine effort, honesty, and accountability to begin to heal.
