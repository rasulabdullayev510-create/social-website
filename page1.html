Origins & Causes

Why Italy invaded Libya, and how Libyans responded

Background: Libya Before Italy

Before Italy took over Libya, the land was part of the Ottoman Empire. It was divided into three regions: Tripolitania in the northwest, Cyrenaica in the northeast, and Fezzan in the south. The people who lived in Libya were mostly Arab-Berber and Muslim, with a way of life based on tribes, farming, and trade — a tradition that had existed for centuries.

The Ottoman Empire did not have a strong hold over Libya, so local leaders and Islamic institutions stepped in to fill that role. The Senussi Brotherhood in Cyrenaica was particularly influential, meaning that Libyans were largely able to govern themselves before the Italian invasion.

Who, What, When, Where, Why

Who

The main power was the Kingdom of Italy, led by figures such as Giovanni Giolitti. They were opposed by the Ottoman Empire and local Libyan resistance groups, especially the Senussi Order led by people like Omar Mukhtar.

What

Italy invaded and colonized Libya, taking control from the Ottoman Empire. This included military conquest, the settlement of Italian civilians, and efforts to reshape the region into an Italian territory.

When

The invasion began in 1911 during the Italo-Turkish War. Italian control continued until World War II, ending around 1943.

Where

The conflict took place across Libya, particularly in the regions of Tripolitania and Cyrenaica.

Why

Italy wanted to expand its empire and compete with other European powers. It sought natural resources, land for settlers, and greater international prestige. Libya was also seen as strategically important due to its position in the Mediterranean, close to Europe.

Economic Causes: Resources and Trade Routes

Italy had been doing business in North Africa for a long time. Italian merchants, banks, and companies had been operating in Tripoli for many decades. By controlling Libya, Italy would gain control over key Mediterranean trade routes, as well as access to farmable land, minerals, and the possibility of oil.

Italian government reports described Libya as a "granary of Rome," echoing ancient Roman claims to the region — even though the reality of Libya's terrain, mostly desert, did not match that description.

Political Causes: Italy's Need for a Colony

Italy became a unified country in 1861 and did not yet have colonies like Britain, France, and other European powers. Italian leaders believed that having colonies was a sign of national greatness, and they wanted Italy to be seen as an equal to the major European empires.

There was also significant pressure from within Italy itself. The government was dealing with widespread poverty, unemployment, and large numbers of Italians emigrating abroad. Leaders believed a colony in North Africa could provide land for settlers, ease the population problem at home, and boost national pride. Italian propaganda called Libya the "fourth shore" — as if it were simply another part of Italy, just across the sea.

Reaction of the Colony

The people of Libya did not accept the Italian invasion quietly. They fought back continuously. When Italy first arrived, many Libyans fought alongside the Ottoman Empire. Even after Italy declared control over the territory, the resistance did not stop — especially in Cyrenaica, where the Senussi Order held strong influence.

Leaders like Omar Mukhtar led the fight against the Italian army for nearly twenty years, using their deep knowledge of the desert landscape to their advantage. Despite Italy's superior weapons, the Libyan fighters were persistent, strategic, and deeply committed to their cause.

Under Benito Mussolini, Italy's response to this resistance became increasingly brutal — including mass imprisonment, executions, and the forced relocation of people into camps. This caused tremendous suffering among the Libyan population.

Overall, the Libyan people never made it easy for Italy to rule. Their organized and sustained opposition — defined by fighters, community leaders, and ordinary people alike — is what truly defines Libya's reaction to the Italian invasion.

Resistance

The resistance to Italian rule was long-lasting and deeply organized. From the very start of the invasion, large numbers of Libyans took up arms — first alongside the Ottoman Empire, and then independently. The Senussi Order played a central role in coordinating this effort, particularly in Cyrenaica.

Omar Mukhtar became the most iconic symbol of this resistance, leading guerrilla warfare against Italian forces for nearly two decades. Even as Italy tightened its grip, many Libyans refused to accept colonial rule. The invasion remained deeply unpopular, and that opposition never fully disappeared — which is why it took Italy so many years, and such extreme measures, to maintain control.
